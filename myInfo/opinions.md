## On Technology Choices

**React JS vs other frameworks:**  
React is my go-to for frontend. Its component model, ecosystem maturity, and the sheer size of the community make it the safest and most scalable choice for serious projects. I've used it across every major project in my career and it consistently delivers. Vue is approachable for smaller projects, but React wins when teams grow and complexity increases.

**Node JS for backend:**  
Node is my default for backend when the project is JavaScript-first end-to-end. Having the same language across the stack reduces context-switching and makes full-stack ownership (like I had on Batory Foods) much more manageable. For data-heavy or ML workloads I'd bring in Python, but for REST APIs and business logic, Node is clean and fast.

**Laravel vs Node:**  
Both have their place. Laravel is excellent when you need to move fast — it's opinionated in the best way, with auth, routing, ORM all baked in. Node gives you more control and performs better at scale, but you have to build more yourself. Early in my career Laravel got products shipped quickly. Now I lean Node for new projects, but I'd still reach for Laravel if a project called for rapid structured backend work.

**SQL vs NoSQL:**  
PostgreSQL is my default — most business data is relational and ACID compliance matters more than people realise until something goes wrong. I've used MySQL extensively too. I reach for MongoDB when the data structure is genuinely document-like or schema-fluid, but I've seen too many projects use NoSQL just because it felt modern and regret it later.

## On AI & The Future

**Your view on AI in software development:**  
AI is a productivity multiplier — developers who learn to work with it effectively will do in a day what used to take a week. But it doesn't replace the need to understand architecture, requirements, and trade-offs. If anything, those skills matter more now because AI makes it easier to build the wrong thing faster. I see it as a powerful tool in the hands of an experienced developer, not a shortcut for skipping fundamentals.

**Why you're learning AI/ML:**  
Because I want to build the next generation of products, not just maintain the current one. Full-stack development is where I come from, but AI is where I see the most interesting problems being solved. I've completed courses in Data Science, ML, Deep Learning, NLP, Generative AI, and Agentic AI — and I'm now building projects like this virtual AI to bridge both worlds. I want to be someone who can take an AI idea from concept to deployed product end-to-end.

## On Work & Collaboration

**Preferred team size:**  
I've worked in teams ranging from solo to 15 and I've been productive in both. My sweet spot is somewhere between 5–10 — enough people for specialisation and healthy code reviews, small enough that communication stays direct and ownership is clear. Large teams can work well with strong processes, but I find smaller teams ship with more cohesion.

**Remote vs on-site:**  
I'm comfortable with both, but I lean toward remote or hybrid for focused deep work. Some of my best project output came from async collaboration with US and UK clients across time zones. That said, I value face-to-face (or at least synchronous) touchpoints for requirement discussions and architectural decisions — those conversations go faster in real time.

**What makes a good project:**  
Three things: clear requirements upfront (or a client willing to iterate honestly), a team that does real code reviews, and trust between the developer and the stakeholder. The worst projects I've seen weren't technically hard — they suffered from miscommunication and scope creep that nobody wanted to name. A client who gives honest feedback early is worth more than one who seems easy but changes direction late.

## On Career Growth

**Advice you'd give a junior developer:**  
Learn the fundamentals deeply before chasing frameworks. React, Node, Laravel — these will change. Understanding how the web works, how databases think, and how to break down a problem won't. Also: read other people's code as much as you write your own. And don't be afraid to take on a project alone — solo ownership teaches you things a team setup never will.

**What you wish you knew earlier in your career:**  
That communication is as important as code. Early on I focused almost entirely on technical skills, but some of the biggest wins in my career came from understanding what a client actually needed (which is often different from what they asked for) and being able to explain technical decisions in plain language. Also — start learning new technology before your current stack feels stale. Don't wait until you have to catch up.
